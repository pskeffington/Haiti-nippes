# Module one topic selection: Haiti Nippes maternal health

## Global health challenge selected

Limited access to safe, timely, and skilled maternal and emergency obstetric care in rural Haiti, with a focused case study of Nippes Department.

## Rationale for selecting the challenge

Haiti's maternal-health burden remains high in the regional context. PAHO's Haiti country profile reports a maternal mortality ratio of 350.4 deaths per 100,000 live births in 2020 and notes that only 65.1 percent of births were attended by skilled health personnel in 2018. The same profile identifies preventable maternal deaths, emergency obstetric care, blood-product availability, rural healthcare accessibility, and maternal and child health within universal health coverage as priority concerns.

Nippes is a useful departmental case because rural access barriers, post-earthquake recovery needs, road disruption, WASH fragility, and health-system limitations intersect in a way that makes maternal survival dependent on more than facility-based clinical care alone. The project therefore frames maternal health as a rural service-delivery and emergency-referral problem, not only as an individual clinical-risk issue.

## Country and subnational focus

The country focus is Haiti. The subnational focus is Nippes Department, including the Miragoane, Anse-a-Veau, and Baraderes arrondissements and their communes.

## Rationale for pairing Haiti and Nippes with maternal health

Haiti offers a clear global-health case in which maternal outcomes are shaped by health-system financing, workforce shortages, fragmented service delivery, disaster exposure, displacement, insecurity, and uneven rural access. Nippes adds a concrete geography for studying these constraints at the department and commune level. This pairing supports a practical final project that can connect demographic context, maternal-health indicators, road access, emergency obstetric referral, WASH conditions, and departmental recovery planning.

## Basic demographics and context to verify

Nippes is one of Haiti's ten departments, with Miragoane as the capital. The working repository profile records an estimated 2015 population of about 342,325 and identifies the department as a coastal and rural area across the Miragoane, Anse-a-Veau, and Baraderes arrondissements. These figures should remain source-tagged against IHSI or another official Haitian statistical source before final submission.

At the national level, PAHO reports Haiti's 2024 population at 11,772,557 and describes persistent social and health precariousness linked to political instability, disasters, and climate-related hazards. PAHO also reports high out-of-pocket spending and low public expenditure on health, both of which are relevant to maternal access and rural referral planning.

## Impacted subpopulations

The primary impacted subpopulation is pregnant and postpartum women in rural Nippes, especially those who need skilled birth attendance, emergency obstetric care, blood transfusion, cesarean delivery, referral transport, postpartum care, or neonatal stabilization.

Secondary impacted groups include adolescent pregnant women, newborns, lactating women, women with hypertensive disorders or hemorrhage risk, women in remote communes with poor road access, displaced women and girls, survivors of sexual violence, and households facing WASH or food-security stressors. UNFPA's 2025 Haiti situation reporting highlights urgent sexual and reproductive health needs among displaced women and girls, pregnant and breastfeeding women, newborns, and women requiring continuity of services.

## Initial research question

How can Haiti's Nippes Department improve rural maternal survival by strengthening skilled birth attendance, emergency obstetric referral, facility readiness, WASH resilience, and commune-level access planning?

## Working thesis

Maternal mortality in rural Haiti is best understood as a systems problem shaped by facility readiness, emergency referral, transportation barriers, WASH conditions, financing constraints, and disaster recovery. In Nippes Department, a feasible policy response should combine community-based prenatal linkage, skilled birth attendance, emergency obstetric transport, blood-product access, facility-readiness monitoring, and source-tagged commune-level access analysis.
